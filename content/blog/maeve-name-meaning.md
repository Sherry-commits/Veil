## What Does the Name Maeve Mean?

The name **Maeve** is one of the oldest, most potent names in the Celtic world — and one whose meaning has no equal in any other naming tradition. In Old Irish, Maeve (Meadhbh) means **"she who intoxicates"** or **"the intoxicating one"** — from the root *medb*, connected to the Proto-Celtic *medwā*, related to the concept of mead (the honey wine of ancient feasts) and the state of divine intoxication it produced.

This is not intoxication in the trivial sense. In the ancient Celtic world, intoxication was associated with **prophetic vision, divine connection, and the power to alter consciousness** — to make others see the world differently simply by being present. Maeve souls carry this energy: they are the people in the room who change the atmosphere by walking in.

## The Origin of the Name Maeve

### Queen Maeve of Connacht

The most famous bearer of this name is **Queen Medb of Connacht** (anglicized as Queen Maeve), one of the most powerful figures in Irish mythology and the central character of the great Ulster Cycle epic, the **Táin Bó Cúailnge** (The Cattle Raid of Cooley).

Maeve was no passive queen. She initiated the great cattle raid herself, led armies, negotiated political marriages with complete self-possession, and confronted the Ulster hero Cú Chulainn with an army she had assembled and commanded. Ancient Irish texts describe her as having seven men as her personal guard simultaneously because "no man without a tremendous shadow can satisfy her" — a line that speaks to her legendary scale of presence and desire.

What the mythology captures is a portrait of **sovereign feminine power** — not power that derives from a man's permission or a social system's approval, but power that is inherent, owned, and expressed without apology.

### Maeve Across Irish History

The name Maeve has been carried by Irish women across centuries as a declaration of that same sovereign energy. It was a name given to girls who were expected to know their own worth — and to insist on it.

In the 20th century, the name spread beyond Ireland through the Irish diaspora and has become one of the most beloved Celtic names internationally — appreciated for its sound (the single syllable has an extraordinary resonance), its rarity relative to its quality, and the quality of woman it tends to produce.
