## What Does the Name David Mean?

The name **David** is the name of a king, a poet, a warrior, and a man described in scripture as having **"a heart after God's own heart"** — and its meaning captures this extraordinary complexity in a single, beautiful word. In Hebrew, David (דָּוִד, *Dawid*) means **"beloved"** or **"friend"** — from the root *dwd* (to love, to be loved).

It is the simplest and perhaps the most profound thing a name can mean. Not "powerful," not "wise," not "victorious" — but *beloved.* David souls are, in the deepest sense, those who are capable of receiving love as fully as they are capable of giving it.

## The Origin of the Name David

### King David of Israel

The biblical David is one of the most fully realized human characters in all of ancient literature. He is not a saint; he is a man — flawed, passionate, gifted, capable of great beauty and great failure, and pursued throughout his life by a love he did not always feel himself worthy of.

His story begins with a shepherd boy, overlooked by his own family when the prophet Samuel comes to anoint a king. God's instruction to Samuel is one of the most quoted passages in the Hebrew Bible: **"Man looks on the outward appearance, but the Lord looks on the heart."**

David is chosen because of what he carries inside.

From there, his story unfolds in extraordinary complexity: the giant-slayer who uses a sling while others reach for swords. The court musician whose playing calms the madness of a king. The warrior whose tactical genius builds an empire. The poet whose Psalms — 73 of the 150 attributed to him — remain, three thousand years later, among the most widely read texts in human history. And the man who, at the height of his power, commits terrible acts and then writes poetry of devastating honesty about what he has done.

David is the human being in full — not the saint, not the monster, but the whole, complicated, beloved creature.

### David Across Cultures

Few names have spread as universally as David. It is among the most common given names across the Western world, from the United States to Israel to Wales (where it is the patron saint's name — the country's flag day, March 1st, is *Dydd Gŵyl Dewi Sant*, Saint David's Day). In Arabic it appears as *Dawud*; in Hebrew it has never left active use; in virtually every language it has found a home.
